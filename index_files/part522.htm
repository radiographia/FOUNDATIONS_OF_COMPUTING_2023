Types of Compression Techniques‌

Three ways to categorize compression techniques are:

1. Lossless or lossy. A compression technique is lossless, if its decompressed data is the same as original data; else, it is lossy (because of loss of originality in reproduced data). Usually, lossy compression techniques achieve higher compression ratios (larger reduction in data volume), but they introduce some distortion in original data quality on decompression of compressed data. In lossy compression, acceptable quality level of decompressed data is application dependent.

2. Symmetrical or asymmetrical. A compression technique is symmetrical, if its compression and decompression processes require roughly the same amount of time; else, it is asymmetrical. In an asymmetrical compression technique, compression time is usually much longer than decompression time. Applications that need to digitize and compress data in real time (such as video conferencing) use symmetrical compression

techniques. On the other hand, those applications, which need to compress the data once and decompress it several times (such as video-on-demand applications), use asymmetrical compression techniques.

3. Software or hardware. A system may implement a compression technique in either hardware or software. As compared to software codecs (COder and DECoder), hardware codecs offer better quality and performance. However, they are costlier and are available only on platforms with costly extended hardware. Software codecs have wider consumer accessibility because of their ease of portability on any platform. Due to performance reason, hardware codecs are suitable for CPU-intensive processes and software codecs are suitable for less CPU-intensive processes.
