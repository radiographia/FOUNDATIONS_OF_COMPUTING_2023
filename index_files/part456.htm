Static and Dynamic Routing‌

Depending on when a routing algorithm specifies the information it uses for making routing decisions, and how frequently it modifies this information, routing algorithms are of two types:

1. Static routing. In static routing (also known as fixed or deterministic routing), system administrators set routing tables (stored at the nodes) and do not change it for long periods, until the network undergoes major modifications. This method is simple and easy to implement. However, it makes poor use of network bandwidth and causes blocking of a packet even when alternative paths are available for its transmission. Hence, it is susceptible to component failures.

2. Dynamic routing. In dynamic routing (also known as adaptive routing), the system automatically keeps updating routing tables with dynamically changing state of the network, such as presence of faulty or congested channels. The method enables use of alternative paths for packet transmissions, making efficient use of network bandwidth and providing resilience to failures. In dynamic routing, however, packets of a message may arrive out of order at the destination node. The method handles this problem by appending a sequence number to each packet and reassembling the packets at the destination node.
