Modulation Techniques‌

The three forms of modulation are amplitude, frequency, and phase.

1. Amplitude Modulation (AM). Amplitude modulation represents two binary values (0 and 1) of digital data by two different amplitudes of the carrier signal, keeping frequency and phase constant. Voice-grade lines use amplitude modulation up to 1200 bps. However, amplitude modulated signals are sensitive to impulse noises that arise due to electrical sparks near transmission lines.

2. Frequency Modulation (FM). Frequency modulation represents two binary values of digital data by two different frequencies, keeping amplitude and phase constant. Also known as frequency shift keying (FSK), this method is less susceptible to error than amplitude modulation. It is also easier to design devices based on FSK because discrimination between two frequencies is simpler than detecting phase changes. Hence, FSK scheme is preferred for medium speed communications (1200 to 2400 bps).

3. Phase Modulation (PM). Phase modulation represents two binary values of digital data by shift in phase of carrier signal. That is, a sine wave with phase = 00 represents a digital 1, and a sine wave with phase = 180 0 represents a digital 0. This technique is more noise resistant and efficient than both AM and FM techniques. For higher-speed transmission of 9600 bps, phase modulation is more reliable and preferred to other methods.
