Basic Requirements‌

To be practically useful, a cryptosystem must fulfill the following basic requirements:

1. It must be easy to use and its encryption and decryption algorithms should be efficient for computer application.

2. There are two methods to achieve security. In the first method, the designer/owner of a cryptosystem keeps the encryption algorithm secret, and the algorithm is complex and difficult to guess. In the second method, the designer/owner of a cryptosystem makes the encryption algorithm public, but keeps the keys secret, and a key is long enough to make it practically impossible to guess. The second method is preferred for practically useful systems. That is, security of a cryptosystem should depend only on secrecy of its keys and not on secrecy of its encryption algorithm.

3. The system must be computationally (practically) secure. That is, determination of Kd must be computationally infeasible for an attacker (also called a cryptanalyst). Strength of a cryptosystem is measured by the level of difficulty (usually measured either by the time or number of elementary operations) of determining Kd. Depending on the amount of information available to an intruder, in a cryptosystem, attacks are mainly of three types - ciphertext only, known plaintext, and chosen plaintext.

In ciphertext-only attack, an intruder is able to intercept ciphertext and tries to derive Kd from the ciphertext. A system that cannot resist a ciphertext-only attack is totally insecure and useless.

In known-plaintext attack, an intruder has considerable amount of both ciphertext and

corresponding plaintext and tries to derive Kd from them. A system that can resist a known-plaintext attack is considered secure.

In chosen-plaintext attack, an intruder has access to ciphertext for any plaintext of his/her choice. The intruder tries to derive Kd by examining several ciphertexts for carefully thought plaintexts of his/her choice. It is most appropriate nowadays to evaluate cryptosystems by their ability to withstand chosen-plaintext attacks.

Another way by which evaluators evaluate a cryptosystem is by test of time. If users of a cryptosystem have not reported any known successful attacks since its publication, and the system has been in use for a significant amount of time (measured in years), the cryptosystem is considered to probably provide pretty good security.
