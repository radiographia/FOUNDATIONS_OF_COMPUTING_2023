Synchronous Transmission‌

In synchronous transmission, a communication system groups characters into blocks, and then adds a header and trailer to each block to convert it into a frame [see Figure 12.15(b)]. The header contains synchronization information, which receiving device uses to set its clock in synchronism with the sender end clock. It also contains information to identify the sender and receiver. A block containing the characters for transmission follows the header. The number of characters in a block may be variable, and may consist of hundreds of characters. A trailer terminates the message characters of a block. The trailer contains an end-of-message character, followed by a check character to aid detection of any transmission error. Hence, in synchronous transmission, a communication system frames an entire block of characters and then transmits it at a time.

Synchronous transmission is suitable for communication between buffered devices (such as between two computers, between a computer and printer, or between a computer and a buffered

terminal). In case of a buffered terminal or printer, logical data block length would be 80 or 132 characters.

Synchronous transmission has much higher data transmission rates than asynchronous transmission, because it eliminates the need for individual start-stop bits for each character. Its main disadvantage, however, is the need for local buffer storage at both sender and receiver ends to assemble blocks, and the need for accurately synchronized clocks at both ends. Hence, synchronous equipment usually cost more.
