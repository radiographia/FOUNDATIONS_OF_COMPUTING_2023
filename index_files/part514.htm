Audio‌

Computer audio deals with tools and technologies for synthesizing, recording, and playing of audio or sound with a computer. Audio is yet another important component of multimedia, because in many cases, sound enables us to understand certain information much better and quicker, and in some cases, sound is the only way to provide the information. For example, in a multimedia application for educating children (our previous example), the designer can add voice

explanation to an animation clip for describing what to look for in the animation that makes a horse's gallop different from a horse's walk. Similarly, an animation clip can show a horse neighing and the designer can add neighing sound to more effectively convey the type of sound horses make. Note that, it is nearly impossible to provide an accurate description of a horse's neigh, lion's roar, or bird's chirp using text, and graphics is of no help in these cases. Hence, sound is the only medium that can accurately provide such information.


Analog and Digital Audio

Audio information travels in natural medium in the form of sound waves that are analog in nature. To enable a computer to deal with audio information, a device must convert sound waves from analog to digital form. Transducer is a device that converts signals from one form to another. For example, microphone is a transducer that converts sound waves to electrical signals, and loudspeaker is a transducer that converts electrical signals to sound waves.

Both analog and digital signals can represent information. Analog signals are continuous tone of smooth fluctuations, while digital signals are discrete values in the form of numbers. A/D (Analog-to-Digital) conversion transforms an analog input (such as voltage) into a series of numeric representation by digitization. D/A (Digital-to-Analog) conversion is the reverse process of transforming a sequence of discrete numbers back into continuous analog signal. A multimedia computer system with audio processing capability requires a sound-board (or sound card), which has:

1. A/D and D/A converters.

2. A connector for a speaker or headphone to allow users to listen to sound recordings.

3. A MIDI input port to input sound from an external MIDI device (such as an electronic

keyboard or drums). MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) is an international standard for digital music that determines the cabling, hardware, and communications protocol for connecting a computer to electronic musical instruments and recording equipment.

4. A MIDI output port to output the recorded sound to the MIDI device.

5. A MIDI synthesizer to translate the information transferred between the computer and the musical instrument.

6. A volume control knob to allow users to control sound volume according to their taste.


Digital Audio Quality

The following three parameters characterize digital audio quality:

1. Sampling rate. Analog-to-digital converter transforms an analog signal to its corresponding digital signal by digitization. Digitization consists of sampling and quantization. Sampling takes snapshots of the analog waveform at periodic intervals, and sampling rate determines how often it takes the snapshots. According to Nyquist theorem, sampling rate must be at least twice the highest frequency component of the analog waveform to reproduce the signal optimally. Higher the sampling rate, truer the sound. Sampling rate is measured in kilohertz (KHz) and its typical values are 8, 11, 22, and 44.1 KHz. For example, sampling rate of CD-quality audio is 44.1 KHz, and that of telephone- quality audio is 8 KHz.

2. Quantization. Quantization determines the measured digital value of the analog waveform at sampling time. Larger the range of numbers for quantization, the more gradations of digitally sampled waveform, and better the audio quality. Quantization is measured in terms of number of bits, and its typical values are 8, 12, and 16 bits.

Figure 14.3 illustrates sampling and quantization of an analog signal. Figure 14.3(a) shows sampling of the analog signal at periodic intervals, and Figures 14.3(b) and (c) show quantization of the sampled values with 2 bits (4 possible quantization levels) and 3 bits (8 possible quantization levels), respectively. In these figures, the closest quantization levels represent the corresponding sampled values. Quantization error is the difference between the value of the closest quantization level and the real analog value. Quantization with more bits provides more quantization levels, resulting in reduced quantization error and increased audio quality.

3. Number of channels. It refers to the number of channels for audio recording. Its typical values are one for mono systems, two for stereo systems, and 5.1 for Dolby surround- sound systems.


Figure 14.3. Sampling and quantization of an analog signal.

Quality, storage, and bandwidth requirements of digital audio depend on the above three parameters. Higher the sampling rate, more the quantization bits, and more the channels, better the quality of digital audio, and higher its storage and bandwidth requirements. For example, an 8 KHz sampling rate, 8 bits quantization, and mono recording produces telephone-quality audio, and requires a bandwidth of 8000 x 8 x 1 = 64 Kb/s. On the other hand, a 44.1 KHz sampling rate, 16 bits quantization, and stereo recording produces CD-quality audio, but requires a bandwidth of 44100 x 16 x 2 = 1.4 Mb/s. In general, an audio clip lasts for several seconds. Hence, audio files are large requiring large storage space. For example, based on the calculations above, a 20 seconds CD-quality audio clip will require 1.4 x 20 = 28 Mb = 3.5 Mbytes of storage. Hence, multimedia computer systems use digital audio compression techniques to reduce storage requirement for storing audio data.


Hardware Requirements for Audio

Normally, modern computers use the following hardware devices for audio processing capability:

1. A sound-board (or sound card) with A/D and D/A converters.

2. An input device (such as a microphone) for audio input to record human voice, or music, or any type of sound. A/D converter of the sound-board digitizes the audio input.

3. An output device (such as a speaker or headphone) for audio output to listen to the recorded sound. D/A converter of the sound-board converts the audio output from digital to analog form.

4. A computer may use MIDI devices for both audio input and audio output.

5. A computer may also enable generation of synthesized sound by using keyboard (as interaction device) and sound sequencer software.

6. Sound editors enable cut and paste of sound sequences to add special effects, and to create new sound sequences from existing sound sequences.

7. Audio mixers enable combining of multiple channels of sound with controls like synchronization points.


Software Requirements for Audio

The following software capabilities are desirable in a multimedia computer system with audio facility:

1. Audio clips. It is a library of audio clips (pre-made sound effects, music, and narrations). A user can directly import an audio clip from this library for use in a multimedia application. This saves lot of time and effort that the user might require otherwise to create or search a similar audio clip. This is also helpful, if a user does not have the facility to create sound sequences. An audio clips library often provides facilities to add a new audio clip or delete an existing audio clip from the library.

2. Audio file importing. An audio file importing software enables a user to import audio files in some standard formats. Common standard audio file formats are .WAV (Windows files), .MID (MIDI files), .VOC, and .INS.

3. Software support for high quality sound. If a multimedia application uses high-quality audio, to reproduce its sound effectively, it is important to have not only the necessary hardware, but also software support for both recording and playback of high-quality audio.

4. Recording and playback capability. It enables users to control recording and playback of an audio sequence. For example, it provides options to ‘pause’ and ‘replay’ a sound sequence.

5. Text-to-speech conversion software. It converts written text into corresponding sound.

6. Speech-to-text conversion software. It converts speech into corresponding text.

7. Voice-recognition software. It enables a user to identify the speaker of a given voice by matching the voice with a set of voices in a database. The database contains details of the speaker of each voice.
